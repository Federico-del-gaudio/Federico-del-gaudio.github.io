Implementation Insights - Combat Systems

Key Learnings from Combat Implementation

During the development of Patch Me If You Can, I learned the importance of iterative design when implementing combat systems. What seemed balanced on paper often felt completely different when players actually engaged with the mechanics.

The most significant insight came from implementing the weapon feedback system. I discovered that visual and audio cues needed to be more exaggerated than initially planned to ensure player comprehension during fast-paced gameplay.

Another crucial learning was the balance between player freedom and gameplay clarity. Allowing players to perform actions while sliding or jumping added depth, but required careful UI design to communicate available options clearly.

Finally, I learned the value of playtesting at every stage. Small adjustments to enemy AI behavior based on player feedback dramatically improved the overall flow and challenge curve of the game.

GIF: Weapon feedback system showing visual hit indicators and enemy reactions.

Technical Implementation Note

The projectile return system was implemented using Unity's raycasting with custom physics layers to ensure smooth interaction with both enemies and environment. This required balancing performance with visual feedback accuracy.

Implementation Insights - Enemy AI

GIF: Enemy AI behavior showing detection, pursuit, and escape mechanics.

Technical Challenge

Implementing the dual conical field of view required custom shader programming and efficient collision detection to maintain performance with multiple active enemies.

AI Behavior Implementation Insights

Developing the enemy AI for Patch Me If You Can taught me valuable lessons about behavior tree design and performance optimization. The puppets needed to feel reactive without overwhelming the player or the game's performance budget.

One key insight was the importance of debug visualization. Creating visual tools to see the AI's field of view, hearing range, and decision-making process was crucial for balancing and bug fixing.

I learned that predictable patterns with controlled randomness created the best player experience. Enemies needed to behave consistently enough for players to learn and counter their behaviors, but with enough variation to avoid feeling robotic.

The integration of environmental interaction into enemy behaviors was particularly challenging but rewarding. Teaching puppets to interact with park attractions as part of their escape behavior added depth to the gameplay loop.

Design Breakdown

Weapon Mechanics

  • Stitching gun combines ranged combat with melee finishing moves
  • Single projectile (needle) that must return to weapon before next shot
  • Shooting available while sliding, jumping, or crouching
  • "E" key to pull hooked enemies toward player
  • Movement slowed during shooting and hooking actions

Detailed mechanics documented in GDD

Enemy Design

  • Puppets wander with dynamic pathfinding
  • Dual conical fields of view for visual detection
  • Spherical hearing range for audio detection
  • Escape behavior with environmental interaction
  • Inspired by Ape Escape movement patterns

Complete AI system detailed in GDD

Feedback Systems

  • Visual hit indicators for successful hits
  • Audio cues for weapon actions and enemy reactions
  • Visual effects for player states (crouching, etc.)
  • Dynamic enemy visual feedback
  • Comprehensive UI feedback system

Full feedback systems documented in GDD
